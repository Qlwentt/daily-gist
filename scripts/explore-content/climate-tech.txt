Subject: Inside Form Energy's Big Google Data Center Deal

Form Energy just announced what would be the largest battery in the world by energy capacity: an iron-air system capable of delivering 300 megawatts of power while storing 30 gigawatt-hours of energy, enough for 100 hours of continuous discharge. The project, developed in partnership with the utility Xcel Energy, will power a new Google data center in Pine Island, Minnesota.

The deal is a landmark moment for long-duration energy storage. Form Energy's technology works through a reversible rusting process: oxygen is pumped into cells filled with thousands of iron pellets, creating rust through oxidation and releasing electrons. When the oxygen is removed, the rust reverts to iron, and the battery recharges. The chemistry uses only abundant, inexpensive materials — iron, air, and water — avoiding the geopolitically risky supply chains that plague lithium-ion batteries.

Google will pay Xcel Energy to build 1,400 megawatts of new wind generation and 200 megawatts of solar alongside the battery storage. The entire package is funded through a contract structure Google developed called a Clean Energy Accelerator Charge, which ensures that regional ratepayers are not left footing the bill. Google is also contributing a $50 million investment toward Xcel Energy's Capacity Connect Program to drive broader grid reliability improvements.

Form expects to begin delivering batteries to the Minnesota site in 2028. The systems will be manufactured at Form Factory 1 in Weirton, West Virginia, which has already started commercial production and is on track to reach 500 megawatts of annual production capacity by the end of that year. The company remains committed to achieving a fully installed system cost below $20 per kilowatt-hour by the end of the decade.

What makes this deal significant beyond its sheer scale is the template it sets. Data centers have become the single largest new source of electricity demand in the United States, and utilities have largely met that demand with natural gas. Google's Minnesota project demonstrates that a combination of wind, solar, and long-duration storage can deliver the round-the-clock reliability that data centers require — without a single gas turbine. If the economics work at this scale, expect every hyperscaler to follow.

The broader context matters too. Google's full-year capital expenditures are projected between $91 billion and $95 billion in 2026, with a significant increase expected in 2027, and energy costs for data centers are a growing share. The race to power AI is becoming inseparable from the race to deploy clean energy. Form Energy's iron-air batteries are suddenly at the center of both.

--- Newsletter: Canary Media ---
Subject: The Grid Storage Industry Set a Wild Goal for 2025 — And Then Crushed It

In 2017, the U.S. grid storage industry made what seemed like an audacious promise: 35 gigawatts of battery capacity installed by 2025. Eight years later, the industry did not just meet that target — it blew past it. The U.S. crossed 35 gigawatts of installed battery capacity in July 2025 and then passed 40 gigawatts in the third quarter, according to data from the American Clean Power Association. By year's end, the country had nearly 45 gigawatts online.

The group of battery vendors, developers, and installers who stood at the margins of the power industry just eight years ago is now second only to solar developers in gigawatts built per year. In 2025 alone, the industry installed roughly 15 gigawatts of new capacity, well above earlier projections. Batteries have gone from a curiosity to a pillar of the American electricity system.

The growth has not been linear. The storage market ran into plenty of false starts along the way. States passed mandates to install batteries but never enforced them. Federal regulators ordered wholesale electricity markets to incorporate storage, but regional implementation dragged on for years. Supply chain disruptions and interconnection delays slowed projects. But the underlying economics kept improving. Lithium iron phosphate battery cells have dropped below $50 per kilowatt-hour, making four-hour grid batteries cost-competitive with natural gas peaker plants in most U.S. markets.

The regional picture is also shifting. While California and Texas dominate the installed base, other regions are catching up fast. In Maine, developer Plus Power recently completed the Cross Town Energy Storage project in Gorham — a 175-megawatt, 350-megawatt-hour lithium iron phosphate battery plant that is now the largest in New England. The $100-million-plus facility, featuring 156 tractor-trailer-sized containers spread across five acres, stores surplus electricity during low demand periods and dispatches it during peaks. During Winter Storm Fern, the facility was 100 percent available with a response time of 250 milliseconds and zero emissions. Maine passed its 2030 storage mandate in 2021; the Gorham project alone knocked out nearly half of it.

Looking ahead to 2026, developers are set to build 86 gigawatts of total power plant capacity — more than ever before — with clean power plants consuming nearly all of that expanded capacity while gas receives minimal new additions. Batteries are increasingly being paired not just with solar farms but with data centers, where they bridge the gap between surging AI-driven power demand and the time it takes to build new grid infrastructure. The storage industry's 2025 performance is not just a milestone. It is a signal that the energy transition has found one of its most reliable workhorses.

--- Newsletter: CTVC (Climate Tech VC) ---
Subject: $40.5 Billion and an 8% Uptick as Power Demand Drives 2025 Climate Tech Investment

Climate tech venture and growth investment totaled $40.5 billion in 2025, up 8 percent year over year. But the headline number masks a more nuanced story. Deal count fell 18 percent, reflecting a market that is consolidating into fewer, larger bets rather than spreading capital across a wide frontier of startups. The uptick looks more like stabilization than a true resurgence, with investors still measured and selective compared to the frenetic pace of 2021 and 2022.

Energy remained the dominant vertical, accounting for 36 percent of total investment and growing 31 percent to $14.4 billion — a three-year high. The driver is unmistakable: surging power demand from data centers, electrification, and reshoring of manufacturing has made energy infrastructure the single most investable theme in climate tech. Growth-stage investment was the clear standout, jumping 78 percent compared to 2024, with deal sizes rising as investors backed companies ready to deploy at scale.

But the early-stage pipeline is thinning. Seed-stage deal count fell 19 percent and Series A dropped 22 percent, as investors pulled back from riskier bets on new entrants. Investor participation declined 19 percent across all climate verticals, with the steepest pullbacks in carbon management (down 47 percent) and transportation (down 31 percent). Early-stage climate tech is entering a maturation phase. Investors have largely finished placing bets in crowded sectors like EVs and carbon removal, and are now waiting to see which companies can actually build and operate at scale.

The fundraising environment tells its own story. New climate-focused funds raised $103 billion in 2025, but the geographic distribution has shifted dramatically. Europe accounted for 54 percent of new fund capital, while the U.S. contributed just 16 percent — a stark reversal driven by weaker climate policy mandates and political uncertainty around IRA implementation. Only 60 percent of targeted fund capital actually closed, leaving $69 billion potentially still in the pipeline.

Perhaps the most telling signal from our investor pulse check: 69 percent of respondents expect capital for first-of-a-kind (FOAK) commercial facilities to shrink through 2026. Over half pointed to FOAK projects as the single toughest development stage to finance. This is the climate tech valley of death — companies that have proven their technology at pilot scale but need hundreds of millions to build their first commercial plant. Many startups are now keeping U.S. headquarters while building FOAK projects abroad, taking advantage of more favorable policy environments and lower deployment costs in Europe, the Middle East, and Asia.

Investors are also hedging against policy whiplash by backing what we call "policy-proof" business models: behind-the-meter energy solutions, SaaS tools for industrial decarbonization, waste heat recovery systems, and energy efficiency retrofits that pencil out on pure cost savings alone, no subsidy required. With uncertainty around tariffs, permitting reform, and the durability of IRA credits, the market is steering hard toward companies that can scale on market economics. The era of climate tech as a policy trade may be ending. What replaces it — climate tech as an energy security and cost optimization play — could prove far more durable.
