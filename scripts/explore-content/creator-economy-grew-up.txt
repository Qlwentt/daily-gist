Subject: The creator middle class is finally here

For years I have been writing about the creator middle class as an aspiration -- something the internet should produce but had not yet delivered. The data is now clear: it is happening, and it looks nothing like what most people expected.

The old creator economy math was brutal. The top 1% of creators captured roughly 90% of total platform revenue. On YouTube in 2020, the top 1% of channels earned an estimated 90% of all ad revenue. On Twitch, the leaked payout data from 2021 showed that the top 1% of streamers earned more than 50% of all platform payouts. On Spotify, the top 1.4% of artists earn 90% of streaming royalties. This was not an economy. It was a lottery with better marketing.

What changed is that the platforms enabling direct monetization -- Substack, Patreon, Gumroad, Teachable, Kajabi -- matured enough to sustain a true middle tier. Substack now has more than 5 million paid subscriptions across its platform, up from 2 million in early 2024. The company reported that more than 10,000 writers earn at least $1,000 per month. That is not a middle class by US income standards, but it is meaningful supplemental income, and the distribution curve is flattening. Patreon crossed $3.5 billion in total creator payouts in 2025, with their median earning creator now making roughly $800 per month -- up from $450 three years ago. The growth is not at the top. It is in the middle.

The most striking trend is the rise of what I call boring creators. The fastest-growing creator categories on YouTube in 2025 were not beauty or gaming. They were finance education, skilled trades, and professional development. A CPA in Ohio named Amanda who runs a channel about small business tax strategy has 340,000 subscribers and earns more from her course sales and consulting leads than most lifestyle influencers with 10x her following. A plumber in Texas named Roger posts 15-minute videos explaining residential plumbing repairs and earns $22,000 per month between YouTube AdSense, affiliate links to tools, and a paid community. A high school chemistry teacher in Georgia sells lesson plan templates on Gumroad and has generated over $400,000 in cumulative sales.

These creators share a common trait: they monetize expertise, not attention. Their audiences are smaller but dramatically more engaged and willing to pay. The conversion rate from free follower to paid subscriber for a niche expertise creator averages 4-7%, compared to 0.5-1% for general entertainment creators. A creator with 50,000 highly engaged followers in a professional niche can realistically earn $150,000 to $300,000 per year. A creator with 2 million followers posting dance videos might earn $40,000.

This is why I keep saying that ownership and audience portability are the most important metrics in the creator economy -- more important than follower count, more important than views. A creator who owns their email list, who can move their audience from one platform to another, who is not dependent on an algorithm to reach the people who already chose to follow them -- that creator has a real business. Everyone else has a job at a company that can fire them without notice by changing a ranking signal.

The platforms are starting to understand this. Substack lets writers export their full subscriber list at any time. Patreon provides direct email access to patrons. YouTube now lets creators with over 10,000 subscribers download their subscriber email list for the first time. These are not small policy changes. They are a fundamental shift in the power dynamic between platforms and creators, and they are happening because creators demanded it and because the platforms that offered portability first -- like Substack -- grew fastest. Competition works.

The creator middle class is not glamorous. It is accountants and plumbers and teachers building sustainable businesses by being genuinely useful. That is exactly what a healthy economy looks like.

--- Newsletter: The Publish Press ---
Subject: Brands are done with traditional ads -- here is where the money is going

The creator economy just crossed $250 billion in total valuation according to Goldman Sachs' latest estimate, up from $104 billion in 2022. That growth is not coming from more creators. It is coming from a massive reallocation of brand marketing budgets away from traditional digital advertising and toward creator partnerships. The shift is accelerating and it is reshaping how companies think about reaching consumers.

The numbers tell the story. The average CPM (cost per thousand impressions) on a creator-produced Instagram Reel or YouTube integration is $15 to $25, depending on the niche. The average CPM on programmatic display advertising is $3 to $7. But here is the part that matters: creator content drives 3-5x higher engagement rates and 2-3x higher conversion rates than equivalent display placements. A study by CreatorIQ analyzing 500,000 branded creator posts found that the average engagement rate on sponsored creator content was 4.2%, compared to 0.9% on brand-owned social posts and 0.35% on display ads. When you adjust for actual business outcomes, creator partnerships are not more expensive. They are cheaper.

The smartest brands figured this out years ago, but what is new in 2025 and 2026 is the structure of these deals. The era of one-off sponsored posts is ending. Brands are moving to long-term partnerships, equity arrangements, and co-ownership models that look more like business relationships than advertising transactions.

Consider the trajectory of Emma Chamberlain. She started as a YouTube creator, then launched Chamberlain Coffee in 2020. By 2025 the brand was in over 7,000 retail locations including Target, Walmart, and Whole Foods, with estimated annual revenue exceeding $100 million. She did not just endorse a coffee brand. She built one, using her audience as the initial distribution channel and her creative sensibility as the brand identity. Investors valued her ownership stake at more than anything she could have earned from a lifetime of sponsorship deals.

MrBeast's Feastables is the most dramatic example. Launched in early 2022, the chocolate brand hit $500 million in annual retail sales by the end of 2025. Hershey reportedly explored acquiring it for north of $1 billion. MrBeast owns the majority of the company. That is a fundamentally different economic proposition than earning $500,000 per sponsored video.

But you do not need to be MrBeast. Ali Abdaal, a productivity YouTuber with about 5 million subscribers, built a course business that generated over $5 million in revenue in 2024. He has since expanded into a media company with a team of 20, producing content across YouTube, a podcast, a newsletter, and paid educational products. His brand partnerships are structured as equity deals where he takes a stake in the companies he promotes rather than a flat fee.

The brand side is evolving too. Unilever announced in early 2026 that it would shift 30% of its digital marketing budget to creator partnerships, up from 12% in 2024. L'Oreal has a dedicated creator ventures fund that takes equity positions in creator-founded beauty brands. Nike's new campaign strategy centers almost entirely on athlete and fitness creator content rather than traditional commercial production.

The implications for the advertising industry are significant. Media buying agencies that built their businesses on programmatic display and search are scrambling to develop creator relationship capabilities. New intermediaries -- creator management platforms like Spotter, Creative Juice, and Kajabi -- are filling the gap, providing creators with upfront capital, analytics, and deal-structuring expertise.

What we are witnessing is the most significant reallocation of marketing dollars since the shift from television to digital. The difference is that this time the money is flowing not just to a new medium but to individuals who own their distribution and their relationship with the audience. That changes everything about how consumer brands are built.

--- Newsletter: Stratechery by Ben Thompson ---
Subject: Platform economics and the creator monetization stack

The most important number in the creator economy right now is $16.5 billion. That is how much YouTube paid to creators in 2025, according to Alphabet's annual report. It is up from $13.5 billion in 2023 and represents roughly 55% of YouTube's total ad revenue being shared with creators through the YouTube Partner Program.

Compare that to TikTok. ByteDance's creator fund -- the $2 billion pool announced in 2020 and distributed over three years -- paid out an average of $0.02 to $0.04 per thousand views. A creator with 10 million views on a TikTok video might earn $200 to $400. The same creator with 10 million views on a YouTube video in a decent CPM category would earn $30,000 to $80,000. TikTok's Creativity Program, launched in 2023 as a replacement for the original fund, improved payouts to roughly $0.50 to $1.00 per thousand qualified views on videos over one minute, but that is still an order of magnitude below YouTube's economics.

This disparity is not accidental. It is structural, and it reveals something important about platform economics and creator sustainability.

YouTube's business model aligns creator and platform incentives. YouTube sells ads against creator content and shares the revenue. The better the content, the more ads YouTube sells, the more both parties earn. This is a positive-sum relationship. YouTube has every reason to help creators succeed because creator success is YouTube success.

TikTok's business model is fundamentally different. TikTok's algorithmic feed means that any individual creator is largely interchangeable. The platform's value proposition to advertisers is not "reach this specific creator's audience" but rather "reach this demographic with this interest profile." The algorithm, not the creator, is the product. This means TikTok has no structural incentive to share meaningful revenue with creators because the creators are inputs, not partners.

This is why short-form video is a trap for creator economics. The format itself -- 15 to 60 seconds, algorithmically distributed, consumed passively in a feed -- is optimized for platform value, not creator value. You cannot build a sustainable business on a format where the platform decides who sees your content, where the audience has no loyalty to you specifically, and where the monetization per view is measured in fractions of a cent.

The most sustainable creator business model in 2026 is the newsletter-podcast bundle. The economics are straightforward. A newsletter with 50,000 free subscribers and a 5% paid conversion rate at $10 per month generates $300,000 in annual subscription revenue. Add a podcast monetized through host-read ads at $50 CPM with 100,000 downloads per episode and two ads per episode, and that is another $10,000 per episode, or roughly $500,000 per year for a weekly show. Total: $800,000 per year from an audience that most social media metrics would consider modest.

The newsletter-podcast bundle works because it combines owned distribution (email), high engagement (long-form audio), premium ad inventory (host-read), and direct monetization (subscriptions). Every component reinforces the others. The newsletter drives podcast listeners. The podcast builds trust that converts free readers to paid subscribers. The subscription revenue provides stability. The ad revenue provides upside.

The other major development reshaping creator economics is AI tooling. Solo creators can now produce content at a quality level that previously required a team. Descript and Riverside have made podcast production essentially push-button. Opus Clip and Vizard automatically extract short-form clips from long-form video with AI-selected highlights. Thumbnail generation tools like ThumblyAI have largely closed the gap between amateur and professional click-through rates. AI-powered SEO tools like vidIQ and TubeBuddy now generate title and tag recommendations that measurably improve discovery.

The effect is deflationary for production costs and expansionary for creator output. A solo creator with the right AI stack can now produce a weekly podcast, a daily newsletter, and three to five short-form video clips per week -- a content volume that would have required a three to four person team two years ago. This matters because it means more of the revenue stays with the creator rather than being consumed by production overhead.

The creator economy grew up. The question now is not whether creators can build sustainable businesses. They can. The question is which platforms and models will define the next era -- and the answer is increasingly clear: the ones that give creators ownership, direct audience relationships, and economics that compound rather than extract.
