--- Newsletter: Ryan Holiday's Reading List Newsletter ---
Subject: Why Most People's Reading Goals Fail (And How to Actually Fix It)

Every January, millions of people set a reading goal. "I'm going to read 52 books this year." By March, they're three books behind schedule, feeling guilty about it, and slowly giving up.

I've read over 300 books in the last five years. Not because I'm fast. Not because I have more free time than you. Because I approach reading completely differently than most people.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: your reading goal is probably making you a worse reader.

**Quit More Books**

The single best thing you can do for your reading life is give yourself permission to quit. I quit probably 40% of the books I start. Sometimes after 50 pages. Sometimes after 5. Life is too short to finish a bad book out of obligation.

There's a concept in economics called the sunk cost fallacy -- the idea that we keep investing in something because of what we've already put in, not because of what we'll get out. This is exactly what happens when you force yourself to finish a book you're not enjoying. You're not being disciplined. You're being irrational.

Nassim Taleb once said that a private library is not an ego-boosting appendage but a research tool. The same applies to your reading list. It's not a checklist to complete. It's a pool to draw from.

**Reading Is a Practice, Not a Performance**

When did reading become a competitive sport? Goodreads challenges. Instagram posts. "I read 100 books last year." Good for you. What do you remember from them?

I'd rather read 20 books deeply than skim 100. Reading is a practice, like meditation or exercise. You don't "win" at meditation by sitting for the most hours. You win by showing up consistently and being present.

This is why I think page count goals beat book count goals. Tell yourself you'll read 25 pages a day. That's it. Some days you'll read more. Some days you'll barely make it. But 25 pages a day is roughly 30-40 books a year, depending on length. And you'll actually absorb what you read.

**The Case for Rereading**

The Stoics didn't read new books every week. Marcus Aurelius returned to the same texts again and again. Seneca wrote that "you should be extending your stay among writers whose genius is unquestionable, deriving constant nourishment from them."

I reread Meditations every year. I reread The War of Art every year. Not because I forgot what's in them, but because I'm different each time I read them. The book hasn't changed. I have. Different passages hit differently depending on what I'm going through.

People think rereading is a waste of time. I think reading a mediocre new book instead of rereading a great one is the real waste.

**The Notecard System**

Here's my secret weapon: a physical box of 4x6 notecards. When I read something that strikes me -- a quote, an idea, a connection -- I write it on a notecard. One idea per card. On the back, I write the source and page number.

I've been doing this for over a decade. I have thousands of cards. When I'm writing, researching, or just thinking through a problem, I flip through them. Ideas collide. Connections form. A quote from Seneca sits next to an observation from Jeff Bezos, and suddenly I see something I never saw before.

This is not a productivity hack. This is how knowledge compounds. You can use Notion or Readwise or whatever app you want. But there's something about the physical act of writing that forces you to process the idea, not just highlight it.

**The Point**

Stop counting books. Start counting what you retain. Give yourself permission to quit, to reread, to slow down. Reading isn't about volume. It's about transformation. Every book you read should change how you see the world, even if only slightly.

Pick up the book on your nightstand tonight. Read 25 pages. Write down one thing that struck you. Do it again tomorrow.

That's the whole system.

- Ryan

--- Newsletter: Austin Kleon's Newsletter ---
Subject: Read Wide, Read Weird: Why Your Reading Diet Needs More Variety

I was at a conference last month and someone asked me what business books I'd recommend. I said: "Honestly? Read a novel."

They looked at me like I'd suggested they eat soup with a fork.

But I meant it. The best ideas I've had -- for my books, my art, my career -- have come not from reading in my field but from reading as far outside it as possible. Poetry collections. Old cookbooks. Biographies of jazz musicians. A field guide to Texas wildflowers.

**The Adjacent Possible**

Stuart Kauffman, the complexity theorist, has this concept called "the adjacent possible." It's originally about biological evolution -- the idea that at any given moment, there's a set of things that are one step away from existing. A lung couldn't evolve without certain preconditions. Innovation happens at the edges of what already exists.

Steven Johnson applied this idea to the history of innovation in his book Where Good Ideas Come From, and I think it applies perfectly to reading. Your next breakthrough idea isn't going to come from the book everyone in your industry is reading. It's going to come from the adjacent possible -- the book that's one step outside your normal orbit.

When you read a book about, say, how ant colonies make decisions, and then you go back to thinking about your team's communication problems, you see things differently. You're not copying ideas. You're cross-pollinating them.

**Fiction Makes You Better at Your Job**

There's actual research on this, by the way. Studies from the University of Toronto found that people who read literary fiction score higher on tests of empathy and social perception. Reading novels literally makes you better at understanding other people.

And what is business, if not understanding other people? What is marketing? What is management? What is design?

I know a CEO who reads a novel every month and credits it with making him a better listener. I know a designer who reads poetry because it teaches her about compression -- how to say the most with the least.

Keith Richards once said, "I've never had a problem with drugs. I've had problems with the police." Similarly, I've never had a problem with reading fiction at work. I've had a problem with people who think reading fiction is a waste of time.

**The Death of the Monoculture**

Here's something that worries me: we're all reading the same things. The same Twitter threads, the same Substack posts, the same books that dominate the algorithm. The monoculture of taste.

When everyone reads the same business book, everyone has the same ideas. When everyone follows the same reading lists, the "adjacent possible" shrinks to nothing. You're not exploring the edges anymore. You're standing in the middle of the crowd.

This is why shared reading still matters -- but shared reading of diverse things. The best book clubs aren't the ones where everyone reads the same bestseller. They're the ones where everyone brings something different and you discover books you never would have found on your own.

**How to Find Good Books (Without Amazon)**

Here's how I actually find books worth reading:

1. **Follow the footnotes.** When a book you love cites another book, go read that one. This is how you build a reading tree instead of a reading list.

2. **Browse physical bookstores.** Algorithms show you more of what you already like. Bookstore shelves introduce you to what you didn't know you'd like. The serendipity is the point.

3. **Ask old people.** Seriously. Ask someone over 70 what their favorite book is. You'll get answers that would never appear on any algorithm.

4. **Read the backs of books.** Not the blurbs. The "also by" section. If an author wrote something great, their other books are worth investigating.

5. **Read dead people.** Books that have survived 50 or 100 or 2,000 years have passed the ultimate filter. They're still in print because they're still worth reading. Don't sleep on the backlist.

The point isn't to read more. It's to read wider. Push into the uncomfortable, the unfamiliar, the weird. That's where the good stuff hides.

Keep reading,
Austin

--- Newsletter: James Clear's 3-2-1 ---
Subject: 3-2-1: On reading and learning, the power of 20 pages, and a question about the book you'd choose again

Happy Thursday,

Here are 3 ideas, 2 quotes, and 1 question to consider this week.

**3 Ideas From Me**

I.

Reading 20 pages a day doesn't sound like much. It's about 20 minutes for most people. But here's the math:

20 pages a day is 600 pages a month. 600 pages a month is roughly 7,200 pages a year. The average book is about 250 pages. That's almost 30 books a year.

The people who read a lot aren't necessarily reading for hours at a time. They're reading a little bit, consistently, for a long time. The magic is in the daily minimum, not the occasional marathon.

Twenty pages. Every day. That's the whole strategy.

II.

If you want to read more, put the book on your pillow in the morning.

This is environment design applied to reading. When you get into bed at night, the book is already there. You have to pick it up to move it. And once it's in your hands, you might as well read a few pages.

Compare this with the usual setup: the book is on a shelf, or in a stack on your desk, or still in the Amazon box. Out of sight, out of mind. You get into bed, grab your phone instead, and scroll for 45 minutes.

You don't need more motivation. You need a better default. Make the book the easiest thing to reach and the phone the hardest. Swap their positions. Put the phone across the room. Put the book on the pillow.

Your environment is an invisible hand that shapes your behavior. Design it for the person you want to become.

III.

There is a difference between reading to finish and reading to understand.

Reading to finish is about completion. You're moving through pages to reach the end. The goal is the last page. You might retain some of it, but that's a side effect, not the purpose.

Reading to understand is about absorption. You pause. You reread a paragraph. You write a note in the margin. You put the book down and stare at the ceiling for a minute because something just clicked. The goal isn't the last page. The goal is the change the book creates inside you.

Most people default to reading to finish. It feels productive. You're making progress. But progress toward what?

The books that changed my life are the ones I read slowly. Some of them took me months. I'd read ten pages, think about them for days, then read ten more. That's not inefficient. That's how understanding works.

**2 Quotes From Others**

I.

Writer and philosopher Henry David Thoreau on the value of morning reading:

"Read the best books first, or you may not have a chance to read them at all."

II.

Novelist and essayist Marilynne Robinson on the internal rewards of reading:

"A good book is an education of the heart. It enlarges your sense of human possibility, of what human nature can be, and of what happens in the world. It is a creator of inwardness."

**1 Question For You**

If you could only reread one book for the rest of your life -- not a new book, but one you've already read -- which would you choose?

And what does your answer tell you about what matters most to you?

Until next week,
James Clear
