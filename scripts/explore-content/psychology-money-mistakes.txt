Subject: Why Smart People Make Terrible Money Decisions

There is a well-known study from the Swedish bank Skandia that tracked the investment returns of Mensa members. These are people with IQs in the top 2%. Their investment returns, on average, underperformed the market. Some of them lost catastrophic amounts on concentrated bets. Being smart is not the same thing as being wise with money.

Intelligence helps you solve math problems. Wisdom helps you sit still when the market drops 30% and every cell in your body screams to sell. These are completely different skills, and they often live in completely different people.

I have met people with finance PhDs who are broke because they over-leveraged themselves chasing alpha. I have met janitors who retired with $8 million because they bought index funds for 40 years and never sold. The janitor is not smarter. The janitor has a better relationship with patience, ego, and enough.

This is the great irony of wealth: it is mostly what you do not see. Wealth is the car you could have bought but did not. The kitchen renovation you skipped. The first-class ticket you exchanged for an economy seat and a fatter brokerage account. We tend to judge wealth by what people spend, but true wealth is the opposite. It is the financial assets that have not yet been converted into stuff.

When you see someone driving a $100,000 car, the only thing you know for certain is that they have $100,000 less than they did before they bought it. Or worse, they have a $1,200 monthly payment and a depreciating asset. You do not know if they are wealthy. You know they spent money. These are very different things.

The reason this is so hard is that spending money is the only way to signal wealth to other people. But the people you are trying to impress are not paying attention. They are too busy thinking about themselves. The entire exercise is a hamster wheel of social signaling that makes you poorer and no happier.

Your relationship with money was largely formed before you turned 18. If you grew up during a recession, you are statistically more risk-averse with investments for the rest of your life. If you grew up during a boom, you tend to take on more risk. This is not rational. This is not a spreadsheet decision. This is a deep psychological pattern shaped by experiences you may not even consciously remember.

A person who grew up with parents who fought about money every night at the dinner table has a fundamentally different emotional relationship with a savings account than someone who grew up in a household where money was never discussed because there was always enough. Neither person is wrong. But they will make completely different decisions with the same information, and they will both believe they are being perfectly rational.

The single most important financial skill is not asset allocation or tax optimization or finding the right stock. It is getting the goalpost to stop moving. Modern capitalism is brilliant at one thing above all else: making you feel like you do not have enough. You get a raise and immediately recalibrate your expectations. You hit your savings goal and set a new, higher one. The finish line moves forward at exactly the pace you run.

A CEO making $10 million a year who needs $11 million to feel successful is objectively poorer than a teacher making $60,000 who has enough. The word "enough" is not popular. It feels like settling. But it might be the most powerful word in personal finance. Not because ambition is bad, but because the inability to say "I have enough" is what causes smart, high-earning people to end up stressed, overextended, and unhappy despite everything they have accomplished.

Wealth, in the end, is the ability to wake up and say: I can do whatever I want today. That is it. That is the whole game.

Subject: The 5 Money Traps Millennials and Gen Z Keep Falling Into (And How to Actually Stop)

Let us talk about the money traps that our generation keeps walking into with our eyes wide open, because recognizing the pattern is the first step to breaking it.

Trap 1: Lifestyle Creep Is Eating You Alive

You got a raise. Congratulations. You also now have a nicer apartment, a better car, and a more expensive gym membership. Your savings rate is exactly the same as it was when you made $15,000 less. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, consumer spending increases almost dollar-for-dollar with income increases for households earning between $50,000 and $150,000. We are not saving our raises. We are spending them in real time.

The insidious thing about lifestyle creep is that each individual upgrade feels small and reasonable. Of course you deserve a one-bedroom instead of a studio. Of course you should not be eating ramen anymore. But these "of course" decisions stack up. A $200 increase here, a $150 increase there, and suddenly your $12,000 raise has been entirely absorbed into a slightly nicer version of the same life. You are on a more expensive treadmill, but you are not any closer to financial freedom.

Trap 2: The "Treat Yourself" Justification Loop

Parks and Rec gave us "Treat Yourself" as a joke. We turned it into a financial philosophy. The average American now spends $314 per month on impulse purchases, according to a 2024 Slickdeals survey. That is $3,768 per year. Invested in an S&P 500 index fund for 30 years at historical average returns, that is roughly $450,000.

The problem is not occasional treats. The problem is that "treat yourself" has become a reflexive justification for any purchase you cannot rationally defend. Had a bad day? Treat yourself. Had a good day? Treat yourself. It is Wednesday? Treat yourself. The emotional spending cycle is real, and it is reinforced every single time you get that tiny dopamine hit from a purchase.

Trap 3: Subscription Stacking

Pull out your phone right now and add up every subscription you pay for. Netflix, Spotify, iCloud, gym, meal kit, meditation app, that news site you forgot you subscribed to, the streaming service you signed up for to watch one show. A 2024 C+R Research study found that the average American spends $219 per month on subscriptions, and most people underestimate their total by 2.5x when asked to guess. That is $2,628 per year on things that automatically drain your account whether you use them or not.

The subscription model is a masterpiece of behavioral economics. A $14.99 monthly charge does not trigger the same pain response as handing someone a $180 bill once a year. Companies know this. They are exploiting the gap between your perception of small recurring charges and their actual cumulative impact.

Trap 4: FOMO Investing

GameStop. Dogecoin. Shiba Inu. Whatever AI penny stock is trending on TikTok this week. The FOMO investing cycle goes like this: you see someone on social media post massive gains. You feel behind. You throw money at the thing. It drops 60%. You sell at a loss. You swear you will never do it again. Six months later, a new thing trends, and the cycle restarts.

A FINRA Foundation study found that 44% of Gen Z investors made their first investment because of social media. Not because of research. Not because of fundamentals. Because someone on the internet posted a screenshot of their gains. Survivorship bias is the silent killer here. For every person who posts their 10x return, there are hundreds who lost money and posted nothing.

Trap 5: Debt Normalization

Here is the most dangerous trap of all. We have collectively decided that debt is just a normal part of adult life. Car payments, credit card balances, student loans, buy-now-pay-later installments. The average American household carries $10,479 in credit card debt at an average interest rate of 22.76%. That is not normal. That is an emergency. But we have been so thoroughly conditioned to see debt as standard operating procedure that carrying a $8,000 balance feels like just another bill.

When 30% of your income goes to servicing debt, you are not building a life. You are renting one. Break the cycle. Pick one trap. Fix it this month. Then move to the next one.

Subject: Vibecession 2.0: Why Your Feelings About Money Have Nothing to Do With the Economy (And Everything to Do With It)

I coined the term "vibecession" back in 2022 to describe the disconnect between how the economy looks on paper and how it actually feels to live in it. Two years later, the gap has only gotten wider, and I think it is worth exploring why.

Let us start with the numbers. GDP grew 2.8% in 2024. The unemployment rate sits at 4.1%. The S&P 500 hit all-time highs. By every traditional macro metric, the economy is not just fine, it is doing well. And yet. The University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment Index has been hovering near levels we normally associate with recessions. A February 2025 Harris poll found that 56% of Americans believe the economy is in a recession. It is not. But more than half the country feels like it is.

So who is right, the data or the people? I think the answer is both, and that the real problem is that "the economy" means fundamentally different things to different demographics.

If you own a home and hold equities, the economy has been spectacular. Home values are up roughly 47% since 2020. The S&P 500 is up over 80% in the same period. Your net worth has ballooned. The economy is great because your assets appreciated.

If you are a renter trying to buy a first home, the economy is a nightmare. The median home price is $420,000. The average 30-year mortgage rate is hovering near 6.8%. A monthly payment on a median-priced home with 10% down is about $2,470, not including taxes and insurance. In 2019, that same home cost $320,000 at a 3.7% rate, which was a $1,330 payment. The monthly cost of owning a home has nearly doubled in five years. That is not vibes. That is math.

Then there is grocery inflation. The headline CPI number has come down to 3.1%. Politicians and economists point to this and say inflation is cooling. And it is, in rate-of-change terms. But here is what people actually experience: eggs are $4.95 a dozen, up from $1.47 in early 2020. A cart of groceries that cost $150 three years ago now costs $210. Prices did not come down. They just stopped going up as fast. The cumulative impact of two years of 8-9% food inflation is still sitting in every grocery receipt, and telling people "inflation is cooling" while their grocery bill is 40% higher than it was feels genuinely insulting.

Social media has made this exponentially worse. TikTok and Instagram create a constant stream of financial comparison that previous generations never experienced. You are not just comparing yourself to your neighbors anymore. You are comparing yourself to curated highlight reels from millions of strangers. A 24-year-old watching someone their age show off a paid-off house and a Porsche does not think "that is an outlier." They think "I am behind." The algorithmic amplification of wealth content creates a systematic distortion of what normal financial progress looks like.

There is also what I call the "little things" problem. Economists track big categories like housing, healthcare, and energy. But daily life is made up of small transactions that have gotten noticeably more expensive in ways that erode your sense of financial stability. A coffee is $7. A sandwich is $16. A movie ticket for two with popcorn is $45. Parking downtown is $25. None of these individually break the bank, but the cumulative feeling of everything costing 30-40% more than it did recently creates a persistent low-grade financial anxiety that no GDP number can address.

What does this mean? It means we need better economic language. "The economy" is not one thing. It is thousands of overlapping micro-economies experienced differently by every demographic, income bracket, and geography. A 58-year-old homeowner in Ohio and a 26-year-old renter in Austin are living in fundamentally different economic realities, and we do them both a disservice by pretending one GDP number captures their experience.

The vibecession is not irrational. It is the completely rational response to living in an economy that is simultaneously great and terrible, depending on where you sit. The data is real. The feelings are also real. And until we get better at holding both of those truths at the same time, the disconnect will only grow.
