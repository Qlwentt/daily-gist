Subject: Humanoid robots are going mainstream in 2026

The robotics industry just hit an inflection point that has been building for years but suddenly feels inevitable. Three major humanoid robot programs announced production milestones in the same week, and the through-line connecting all of them is the same thing that made ChatGPT possible: foundation models.

Figure AI announced that its Figure 02 humanoid robots are now operating on BMW's Spartanburg assembly line in South Carolina, performing wire harness insertion and small-parts kitting alongside human workers. This is not a pilot. BMW has deployed 47 units across two production lines, with plans to scale to 200 by Q4. The robots work standard eight-hour shifts and handle tasks that previously required workers to reach into awkward spaces inside vehicle frames, a category BMW flagged as its highest ergonomic injury risk. Figure raised $675 million at a $2.6 billion valuation last year, and CEO Brett Adcock says they are already in discussions with three other automotive OEMs. The economics are hard to argue with: Figure quotes an all-in cost of $15 per hour of operation, compared to $45 to $55 per hour for equivalent human labor including benefits and injury costs.

Tesla showed off Optimus Gen 3 at a private investor event in Austin. The new generation is 30% lighter than Gen 2 at 56 kilograms, has 28 degrees of freedom in each hand, and demonstrated folding laundry, sorting packages by label, and navigating around unpredictably moving obstacles. Elon Musk repeated his claim that Optimus will eventually be Tesla's most valuable product, projecting unit costs below $20,000 at scale. The most notable improvement is locomotion: Gen 3 walked over uneven gravel, stepped over a low barrier, and recovered from a shove without falling. Tesla says it is targeting limited deployment in its own Gigafactories by early 2027, with third-party sales beginning the following year.

Meanwhile, 1X Technologies shipped its first 50 NEO Beta units to early-access homes in Norway. NEO is explicitly designed for domestic environments rather than factories. It stands 5 foot 8, weighs 66 pounds, and moves on a wheeled base rather than legs, which simplifies balance but limits terrain. The beta users are testing it on household tasks: loading dishwashers, picking up clutter, retrieving items from shelves. 1X, backed by $125 million from OpenAI's startup fund and Tiger Global, is treating this like a software product. Over-the-air updates push new capabilities weekly. CEO Bernt Bornich says the biggest surprise from beta testing is how quickly users start talking to the robot conversationally, even though it only responds with simple status phrases.

But the real story behind all three is not the hardware. It is the emergence of foundation models for robotics. Google DeepMind's RT-X, trained on data from 22 different robot embodiments across 21 institutions, demonstrated last year that a single model can generalize manipulation skills across robot types it has never seen. The model takes natural language instructions and camera input, then outputs motor commands. It does not need task-specific programming. You tell it to pick up the red cup and put it in the sink, and it figures out the grasp, the trajectory, and the placement.

This is the paradigm shift. For decades, robotics meant painstaking programming of every movement for every task in every environment. Industrial robots like those from FANUC and ABB are incredibly precise but fundamentally brittle. They do one thing perfectly in a controlled setting. Foundation models flip that equation. The robot does many things adequately in messy settings, and it gets better with more data and more compute. That trade-off is exactly what makes humanoid robots viable outside factories for the first time.

Toyota Research Institute's Diffusion Policy work showed that robots can learn dexterous tasks from as few as 15 human demonstrations, using diffusion models to generate smooth, naturalistic motor plans. The approach works for tasks as varied as pouring liquids, flipping pancakes, and plugging in USB cables. Combine that with RT-X-style generalization, and you start to see a path to robots that learn the way humans do: by watching and trying, not by being programmed.

The funding numbers tell you where the smart money thinks this is going. Robotics startups raised $11.7 billion globally in 2025, up from $7.2 billion in 2024. Humanoid-specific companies accounted for $4.1 billion of that total, a category that barely existed three years ago. We are watching the robotics industry's iPhone moment unfold in real time.

--- Newsletter: IEEE Spectrum ---
Subject: The technical breakthroughs that made the 2026 robotics wave possible

The current wave of humanoid robot announcements can feel like hype to anyone who remembers previous robotics booms that fizzled. But the underlying technical progress is real, measurable, and distinct from what came before. Four converging advances explain why this time is different.

The first is sim-to-real transfer learning, which has gone from a research curiosity to a production methodology. The core idea is straightforward: train a robot's control policy in a physics simulator where you can run millions of trials cheaply and quickly, then transfer the learned behavior to a physical robot. The hard part has always been the reality gap, the mismatch between simulated physics and the messy real world. NVIDIA's Isaac Sim and Google DeepMind's MuJoCo have narrowed that gap dramatically. Domain randomization, where you deliberately vary friction, lighting, object mass, and sensor noise during simulation, produces policies that are robust to real-world variation. Agility Robotics reported that its Digit robot's warehouse picking policy required only 12 hours of real-world fine-tuning after 48 hours of simulated training, down from weeks of real-world training required two years ago. The economics of sim-to-real are transformative: simulated training on a GPU cluster costs roughly $0.30 per hour of equivalent robot experience, compared to $150 per hour for real-world training with a human supervisor.

The second breakthrough is in dexterous manipulation. Robot hands have historically been the weakest link. Most industrial robots use simple parallel-jaw grippers that can pick up rigid objects but fail on anything soft, deformable, or oddly shaped. The new generation of humanoid hands, from companies like Shadow Robot, Wonik Robotics, and Tesla's in-house team, feature 16 to 28 degrees of freedom with force-torque sensors on every fingertip. But hardware alone was not enough. The control problem for a 22-DOF hand manipulating a deformable object in real time is computationally intractable with classical methods. Learned policies, particularly those using the diffusion-based approaches pioneered by Toyota Research Institute and Columbia's REAL Lab, have cracked this. These models generate smooth, human-like manipulation trajectories from visual input alone, without requiring precise object models. In benchmark tests, learned dexterous policies now succeed at 87% of previously unsolved manipulation tasks in the YCB object set, including tasks like opening a mustard bottle, threading a nut onto a bolt, and reorienting a power drill for use.

The third factor is the plummeting cost of the physical components. High-quality brushless DC actuators that cost $1,200 per unit in 2023 now cost $720, a 40% reduction driven by manufacturing scale in China and competition among suppliers like Maxon, T-Motor, and Unitree's vertically integrated production. Six-axis force-torque sensors dropped from $3,500 to $1,800 in the same period. LiDAR units suitable for indoor navigation fell below $100 for the first time. The bill of materials for a research-grade humanoid torso with two dexterous arms has dropped from approximately $380,000 in 2023 to $210,000 today, and companies building at production scale like Unitree and Figure are targeting sub-$50,000 full-body platforms. This cost curve mirrors what happened with drones between 2012 and 2018, and it unlocks experimentation by startups that previously could not afford hardware development.

The fourth is the integration of large language models and vision-language models as high-level planners for robotic systems. Google DeepMind's RT-2 demonstrated in 2023 that a vision-language-action model could translate natural language commands into robotic actions. The successor systems, RT-X and RT-H, extended this to cross-embodiment generalization and human-in-the-loop correction. The practical impact is that robots no longer need task-specific state machines. A warehouse robot that encounters an unexpected object orientation can reason about what to do rather than halting and waiting for a human. This is the difference between a robot that works in a demo and a robot that works in deployment.

Despite this progress, significant hard problems remain. Unstructured environments are still the primary failure mode. A robot trained in simulation and fine-tuned in a controlled warehouse struggles when the floor is wet, the lighting changes drastically, or objects are stacked in configurations never encountered during training. Battery life constrains humanoid robots to four to six hours of active operation with current lithium-ion technology, far short of a full work shift without charging breaks. Safety certification is perhaps the most underappreciated bottleneck. There is no established standard for certifying a 60-kilogram humanoid robot to operate in shared spaces with humans. ISO 10218 covers industrial robots behind cages, and ISO/TS 15066 covers collaborative robots bolted to workstations, but a mobile humanoid that walks through a factory falls into a regulatory gap. UL and TUV are developing new frameworks, but certification timelines of 18 to 24 months will constrain deployment speed regardless of technical readiness.

The honest assessment is that humanoid robots in 2026 are roughly where autonomous vehicles were in 2018: technically impressive, commercially nascent, and two to three years from widespread deployment. The difference is that the robotics industry learned from AV's mistakes. The leading companies are targeting constrained environments first, factories and warehouses, rather than promising the open world. That is the right strategy, and it is why this wave is more likely to deliver than previous ones.

--- Newsletter: ARK Invest's Big Ideas ---
Subject: The $24 trillion case for humanoid robots

Humanoid robots represent what we believe is the largest economic opportunity of the next two decades. Our updated models project a $24 trillion addressable market by 2040, making robotics the single largest investment theme in our Big Ideas 2026 report. The comparison we keep returning to is electric vehicles in 2017: the technology worked, costs were falling on a predictable curve, and most investors still thought it was a niche.

The economic logic starts with labor costs. Manufacturing labor in the United States costs an average of $47.50 per hour when you include wages, benefits, workers compensation insurance, and turnover costs. Warehousing and logistics labor costs $38 per hour on the same basis. Eldercare, one of the fastest-growing labor categories globally, costs $32 per hour for home health aides and substantially more for skilled nursing. These are precisely the sectors where humanoid robots will deploy first, because the tasks are physically demanding, repetitive, and increasingly difficult to staff. The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects 1.1 million unfilled manufacturing positions in the United States by 2028 and 600,000 unfilled home health aide positions. Robots are not displacing workers in these categories. They are filling roles that cannot be filled.

Our cost model for humanoid robots projects an all-in operating cost of $5 to $8 per hour by 2030, including amortized hardware cost, energy, maintenance, and software licensing. That assumes a unit price of $25,000 to $50,000, a five-year useful life, and 16 hours of operation per day. At $6 per hour, a humanoid robot is 87% cheaper than a US manufacturing worker and 82% cheaper than a warehouse worker. Even in lower-wage markets like Mexico and Southeast Asia, the crossover point arrives by 2032 in our base case.

The cost curve for the underlying hardware is following Wright's Law with remarkable consistency. For every cumulative doubling of humanoid robot production, we estimate unit costs decline by 18 to 22 percent. Unitree's H1 shipped at $90,000 in late 2024 and the updated H1-2 is priced at $58,000 today. Figure AI's cost per unit dropped 35 percent between its first and second production runs. Tesla has not disclosed Optimus unit costs but has stated publicly that it is targeting below $20,000 at volume, consistent with its track record of aggressive cost reduction through vertical integration and manufacturing scale.

The comparison to EVs in 2017 is instructive. In 2017, global EV sales were approximately 1.2 million units, representing about 1.3% of total vehicle sales. Most institutional investors viewed EVs as a niche product for environmentally motivated consumers. Battery costs had fallen from $1,100 per kilowatt-hour in 2010 to $209 in 2017, but skeptics argued they would plateau. Instead, costs fell to $139 by 2020 and $115 by 2024, crossing the threshold where EVs reached purchase-price parity with internal combustion vehicles in most segments. The result was an S-curve adoption pattern: slow growth through 2019, followed by exponential growth that brought global EV sales to 18.5 million units in 2025, roughly 22% of total vehicle sales.

We see humanoid robots on a nearly identical trajectory, shifted forward by about eight years. Global humanoid robot shipments in 2025 were approximately 4,200 units, almost entirely to automotive and logistics customers. Our base case projects 35,000 units in 2027, 280,000 in 2030, and 7.5 million in 2035. The S-curve inflection occurs around 2029 to 2030, when unit costs cross below $30,000 and reliability metrics, measured as mean time between failures, exceed 2,000 hours.

The companies positioned to capture this market fall into three tiers. The platform companies building full humanoid systems include Tesla, Figure AI, 1X Technologies, Agility Robotics, Unitree, and Apptronik. Tesla has structural advantages in manufacturing scale, vertical integration, and access to real-world data from its vehicle fleet, but Figure AI has moved fastest to commercial deployment through its BMW partnership. In the second tier are the foundation model companies providing the intelligence layer: Google DeepMind with RT-X, OpenAI through its investment in 1X and its own robotics research, and Nvidia with its Isaac robotics simulation platform and GR00T foundation model for humanoid robots. These companies capture value regardless of which hardware platform wins. The third tier is the component supply chain: actuator manufacturers, sensor companies, and battery suppliers that sell to all platforms.

Our conviction is highest in the foundation model layer. Hardware will commoditize, just as smartphone hardware commoditized after 2012 while iOS and Android captured the value. The company that builds the dominant robotics foundation model, a general-purpose model that can control any humanoid body across any task, will occupy a position analogous to what Google's Android became for mobile. Nvidia is making the most aggressive play for this position with GR00T, but Google DeepMind has the strongest research pedigree, and Tesla has the most proprietary real-world training data.

We are adding humanoid robotics exposure across our flagship ARK Innovation ETF and our Autonomous Technology and Robotics ETF. Our five-year expected return for the robotics theme is 45% annualized, driven by the combination of cost deflation, labor shortage tailwinds, and the typical market underestimation of exponential adoption curves. The market priced EVs for linear growth in 2017 and was wrong by an order of magnitude. We believe the same mispricing exists in robotics today.
