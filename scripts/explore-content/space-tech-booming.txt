Subject: The commercial space economy just crossed $500B — and it's accelerating

The commercial space economy hit $546 billion in 2025, according to the Space Foundation's latest quarterly report, and the trajectory looks nothing like the gradual growth curves people drew five years ago. This is not a speculative market anymore. Space is infrastructure.

Start with the obvious. SpaceX launched 234 times in 2025, more than every other launch provider on Earth combined. That number sounds absurd until you realize they are on pace for over 260 in 2026. Falcon 9 has become the 737 of space -- a workhorse so reliable that individual launches barely make news anymore. Boosters are routinely flying their 25th or 30th mission. The company recovered its 400th booster in November. Starship completed six successful orbital flights in the second half of the year, with full stack reusability demonstrated twice. The marginal cost of getting a kilogram to low Earth orbit on Starship is approaching $200, down from $2,720 on Falcon 9 and roughly $54,500 on the Space Shuttle.

But the story that matters more for the industry is what is happening below SpaceX. Rocket Lab's Neutron rocket entered service in October 2025 with a successful maiden flight carrying a test payload to a 500 km sun-synchronous orbit. Neutron slots into the medium-lift category -- 13 metric tons to LEO, reusable first stage, designed for constellation deployment. Rocket Lab has already booked $1.2 billion in Neutron contracts, with Telesat, the Pentagon, and a still-unnamed commercial constellation among the early customers. Peter Beck told investors on the Q4 earnings call that Neutron will fly eight times in 2026 and that the company expects to achieve first-stage reuse by the fourth mission.

Relativity Space, after years of delays, successfully launched Terran R in December. The 3D-printed medium-lift vehicle reached orbit on its second attempt, validating the additive manufacturing approach that skeptics had questioned since the company's founding. Stoke Space is close behind, with its fully reusable upper stage completing a powered landing test in January.

The satellite side of the market is where the real volume growth is happening. Yes, Starlink dominates with over 7,400 satellites in orbit and 5 million subscribers generating an estimated $8.6 billion in annual revenue. But the broader small satellite market has exploded. Planet Labs operates 200+ Earth observation satellites and just signed a $230 million contract with the National Reconnaissance Office. Spire Global runs 180 satellites collecting weather, maritime, and aviation data. Amazon's Project Kuiper has deployed its first 600 satellites and is ramping aggressively toward its 3,236-satellite constellation, with launch contracts across ULA, Arianespace, and Blue Origin.

The pattern is clear: space is becoming a utilities business. Satellite broadband, Earth observation, weather monitoring, GPS augmentation, in-orbit computing -- these are recurring revenue infrastructure plays, not moonshot bets. Morgan Stanley's space team raised their 2030 market forecast to $1.1 trillion, driven primarily by satellite services rather than launch. The fastest growing sub-sectors are direct-to-device satellite connectivity (AST SpaceMobile just activated service on 45 satellites), space-based cloud computing (Lumen Orbit raised a $310 million Series B), and synthetic aperture radar (Capella Space, Umbra, and ICEYE are all scaling).

When the boring money shows up -- insurance companies pricing space assets, banks lending against satellite constellations, pension funds investing in space infrastructure REITs -- you know the industry has crossed a threshold. That is exactly what is happening right now.

--- Newsletter: CNBC Space Newsletter ---
Subject: Space defense is the hottest sector in VC — here's who's getting funded and why

Move over cyber. The hottest defense sector in venture capital right now is space, and the funding numbers prove it. Space defense startups raised $4.8 billion in 2025, up 140% from 2023, according to Space Capital's Q4 report. The sector attracted more VC dollars than autonomous vehicles for the first time.

Anduril Industries, already valued at $28 billion after its $3 billion Series F in August 2025, quietly stood up a dedicated space division in September. The unit is led by a former Space Force acquisitions officer and is focused on what the company calls "mesh space defense" -- networks of small, cheap satellites that can detect, track, and potentially counter adversary systems in orbit. Anduril has not disclosed specific contracts, but multiple sources confirmed a classified program with the Space Development Agency worth north of $500 million. Palmer Luckey told our team at CES that space is "the most underdefended domain in the DoD portfolio" and that Anduril sees it as a multi-billion dollar line of business within three years.

True Anomaly has emerged as one of the most closely watched startups in the space. The Colorado-based company raised a $250 million Series C in January 2026 at a $2.1 billion valuation, co-led by Andreessen Horowitz and U.S. Innovative Technology Fund. True Anomaly builds satellite inspection and tracking spacecraft -- essentially, satellites that can rendezvous with and observe other satellites to determine their capabilities and intent. Their Jackal autonomous vehicle has completed three on-orbit demonstration missions, performing proximity operations within 100 meters of target objects. The company also sells Sightline, a software platform for space domain awareness that fuses data from ground-based radars, telescopes, and space-based sensors into a unified operating picture. The Space Force signed a $180 million contract for Sightline in November, making it one of the largest software deals in Space Force history.

The bigger story here is the Pentagon's decisive shift toward commercial space procurement. The Space Development Agency, which reports to the Space Force, has awarded over $6 billion in contracts to commercial providers since its founding, with $2.3 billion in 2025 alone. SDA's Proliferated Warfighter Space Architecture -- a mesh network of hundreds of small satellites in low Earth orbit providing missile tracking, communications, and data transport -- is being built almost entirely by commercial companies. L3Harris, Northrop Grumman, York Space Systems, and Lockheed Martin are the prime contractors, but the components and subsystems come from startups.

Why is this happening now? Three catalysts. First, China and Russia have demonstrated anti-satellite capabilities that expose the vulnerability of the traditional approach -- a few exquisite, billion-dollar satellites in geosynchronous orbit. A distributed architecture with hundreds of cheap satellites is far more resilient. Second, launch costs have dropped to the point where replacing destroyed satellites is economically viable, which changes the calculus on space warfare entirely. Third, the commercial space ecosystem has matured enough to deliver military-grade capabilities at commercial speed and pricing.

Space domain awareness -- knowing what every object in orbit is, what it is doing, and who controls it -- is being called the next cybersecurity by multiple Pentagon officials. General Chance Saltzman, the Chief of Space Operations, said at the Reagan National Defense Forum that "space domain awareness is to space superiority what cyber intelligence is to information warfare." LeoLabs, which operates a network of ground-based phased array radars tracking over 250,000 objects in LEO, raised a $120 million Series D. Slingshot Aerospace, which builds AI-powered space traffic management software, raised $80 million.

The investment thesis is straightforward: every major military power is moving capabilities into space, space is becoming contested, and the companies building the tools to operate in a contested space environment will be essential national security assets. That is why defense-focused space startups are commanding premium valuations and why we expect this sector to accelerate through 2027.

--- Newsletter: The Orbital Index ---
Subject: The technical breakthroughs that made space cheap — a quantitative look

We spend a lot of time in this newsletter tracking what gets launched. This week, we want to step back and look at why launching things has gotten so radically, almost unbelievably cheap -- and what that unlocks.

The cost per kilogram to low Earth orbit has undergone one of the most dramatic price collapses in the history of any industry. In 2010, the cheapest option was a Falcon 9 v1.0 at roughly $4,600/kg. Today, a flight-proven Falcon 9 Block 5 delivers cargo to LEO for approximately $1,500/kg on a rideshare mission. Starship, based on SpaceX's published targets and observed flight costs, is approaching $200/kg with full reusability -- a 95% reduction from 2010 prices. For context, the Space Shuttle cost approximately $54,500/kg. The Saturn V was around $175,000/kg in 2025 dollars.

This is not just a SpaceX story. Rocket Lab's Electron pioneered dedicated small satellite launch at $25,000/kg, which sounds expensive until you consider that small sat operators were previously paying $40,000-$60,000/kg as secondary payloads on vehicles optimized for large primary customers, with no control over orbit, schedule, or deployment parameters. Electron has completed 58 successful flights. Neutron, now operational, targets $5,000/kg for dedicated missions and around $2,800/kg for rideshare -- competitive with Falcon 9 for medium payloads while offering schedule flexibility.

The key technical driver is reusability economics. A Falcon 9 first stage costs roughly $28 million to manufacture. If you fly it once, that cost is amortized over one mission. Fly it 20 times, and the per-flight hardware cost drops to $1.4 million plus refurbishment (estimated at $1-2 million per flight). SpaceX is now routinely flying boosters 25-30 times. Booster B1061 holds the record at 27 flights. The company has stated that Block 5 boosters are designed for 40 flights with minimal refurbishment. This transforms launch economics from a manufacturing business to an operations business -- more like an airline than a rocket factory.

Beyond launch, the real excitement in the technical community is on-orbit servicing, assembly, and manufacturing. Astroscale completed the first commercial debris removal demonstration in 2025, using its ADRAS-J spacecraft to rendezvous with and de-orbit a spent Japanese rocket upper stage. Orbit Fab has deployed three fuel depots in LEO and completed the first commercial on-orbit refueling, transferring hydrazine to a Lockheed Martin satellite. This is foundational technology -- if you can refuel satellites, you can extend their operational lives from 15 years to 30+, which completely changes the economics of geostationary communications satellites that cost $300-500 million each.

Vast Space is on track for its Haven-1 commercial space station launch in late 2026 on a Falcon Heavy. The single-module station will support four crew members for up to 30 days and is designed as a proving ground for the larger Haven-2 multi-module station planned for 2028. Vast raised a $710 million Series B in May 2025, and has signed agreements with NASA, the Italian Space Agency, and four private customers. Axiom Space has attached its first commercial module to the ISS and plans to detach it as a free-flying station when ISS is decommissioned around 2030.

The cislunar economy -- the economic zone between Earth orbit and the lunar surface -- is transitioning from PowerPoint to hardware. Intuitive Machines landed its third lunar mission in January 2026, delivering a NASA-funded ice mining demonstration payload to the lunar south pole. ispace completed its first successful lunar landing in September 2025 after a failed attempt in 2023. Astrobotic is preparing its second Griffin lander mission. These are not science missions in the traditional sense -- they are logistics demonstrations, proving that commercial companies can deliver cargo to the lunar surface reliably and repeatedly.

The numbers that matter: 224 orbital launch attempts in 2025 (up from 186 in 2024 and 95 in 2020). 2,864 satellites deployed. Global launch revenue estimated at $9.2 billion. The total addressable market for satellite services -- broadband, Earth observation, navigation, IoT -- is projected at $285 billion by 2030. Private investment in space companies reached $17.1 billion in 2025, the second highest year ever after the SPAC-fueled 2021.

The bottom line is that space is cheap enough to be boring, and boring is where the money is. When launch costs drop below a threshold, entirely new categories of space application become viable -- just as cheap cloud computing enabled SaaS. We are in the early innings of that wave, and the technical foundations are now solid enough to support it.
